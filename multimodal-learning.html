Multimodal Learning

Communicating through sounds, images, gestures, stories, and more!

Communication is so much more than just words! Children naturally use multiple modes—sounds, facial expressions, gestures, images, music, movement, and more—to express themselves and understand the world. This rich, multimodal approach to communication supports language development and helps children become creative, confident communicators.

What is Multimodal Communication?

Understanding how children use multiple modes to communicate

Multimodal communication means using different "modes" or ways of making meaning together. From birth, children naturally combine multiple modes—they might point while making sounds, use facial expressions while speaking, or act out stories with their bodies. This is not only normal, it's incredibly sophisticated!

The Different Modes of Communication:

Linguistic Mode

Spoken and written language

  • Words, sentences, vocabulary
  • Grammar and syntax
  • Oral storytelling
  • Written text

Audio Mode

Sounds and music

  • Tone of voice, pitch, volume
  • Music and rhythm
  • Environmental sounds
  • Sound effects

Visual Mode

Images and visual elements

  • Pictures, photographs, illustrations
  • Colors, shapes, patterns
  • Facial expressions
  • Visual symbols and icons

Gestural Mode

Body language and movement

  • Gestures and pointing
  • Body movements and posture
  • Sign language
  • Eye contact and gaze

Spatial Mode

Physical arrangement and space

  • Physical proximity and distance
  • Layout and organization
  • Use of space in play and interaction
  • Environmental design
Key Point: Children rarely use just one mode at a time! When a toddler says "up!" while reaching their arms toward you, they're combining linguistic, gestural, and spatial modes. This multimodal approach is natural, effective, and should be encouraged!

Why Multimodal Communication Matters

The benefits of using multiple modes

Benefits for Children:

Enhanced Understanding

When children receive information through multiple modes (hearing words + seeing pictures + using gestures), they understand and remember better

More Ways to Express

Children who don't yet have extensive vocabulary can still communicate complex ideas through gestures, drawings, and dramatic play

Inclusive Communication

Multimodal approaches support children with diverse learning styles, language backgrounds, and abilities (including Deaf children, children learning English, and children with disabilities)

Creativity and Critical Thinking

Choosing how to represent ideas (through art? drama? words? music?) develops creative thinking and decision-making skills

Literacy Development

Understanding that meaning can be made in multiple ways prepares children to "read" and interpret different kinds of texts (books, websites, videos, signs, etc.)

Engagement and Motivation

Children are naturally more engaged when they can use their whole bodies, voices, and creativity to learn and communicate

Multimodal Language Activities

Practical ideas for each mode

Sound & Music Activities

Singing Songs

Sing nursery rhymes, action songs, and cultural songs together. Add movements and props!

Try: "Old MacDonald" with toy animals, "The Wheels on the Bus" with actions, "Twinkle Twinkle" with twinkling hand movements

Making Music

Use instruments (or homemade shakers!) to explore rhythm, tempo, and sound patterns

Try: Clap rhythms for your child to copy, march to drum beats, create "loud" and "quiet" music

Sound Exploration

Explore environmental sounds and voice variations

Try: Animal sounds, vehicle sounds, weather sounds, silly voice variations (high, low, fast, slow)

Musical Stories

Add sound effects and musical elements to storytelling

Try: Use instruments or voice sounds to represent characters (thunder drum for giant, tinkling bells for fairy)

Gesture & Movement Activities

Action Songs & Rhymes

Songs that include specific movements and gestures

Try: "Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes," "If You're Happy and You Know It," "Incy Wincy Spider"

Dramatic Play

Act out stories, scenarios, and daily routines

Try: Pretend to be animals, act out going to the shops, use dress-up clothes and props

Dance & Creative Movement

Express feelings and ideas through movement

Try: "Move like a butterfly," "Show me happy/sad dancing," "Move slowly like a turtle, fast like a rabbit"

Baby Signs & Gestures

Use simple signs and gestures to support communication

Try: Signs for "more," "all done," "milk," "help"—use consistently with words

Visual & Image Activities

Picture Books

Books with rich illustrations support comprehension and vocabulary

Try: Wordless picture books (child tells the story!), books with detailed illustrations to explore, photo books of familiar objects

Drawing & Art

Children express ideas and stories through drawing, painting, and creating

Try: "Draw a picture of your family," create story illustrations, use playdough to make story characters

Photo Activities

Use photos to tell stories and build vocabulary

Try: Make family photo books, sequence photos of daily routines, create "I went to..." photo stories

Visual Supports

Use pictures and symbols to support understanding

Try: Visual schedules, choice boards with pictures, labeled storage bins with pictures and words

Storytelling & Narrative Activities

Interactive Read-Alouds

Read books with expression, voices, and engagement

Try: Use different voices for characters, add sound effects, pause for predictions, encourage child participation

Oral Storytelling

Tell stories without books—from memory or made up!

Try: Family stories ("When I was little..."), traditional tales from your culture, silly made-up adventures

Story Retelling

Encourage children to retell familiar stories in their own words

Try: Use props and toys to act out stories, sequence picture cards, "Tell me what happened in the story"

Puppet Shows

Use puppets to tell stories and have conversations

Try: Make simple sock puppets, use stuffed toys as characters, create a puppet theatre from a box

Digital Multimodal Learning

Using technology thoughtfully

While face-to-face interaction is best for language development, digital resources can complement learning when used thoughtfully and in moderation. The key is CO-VIEWING—watching or using technology together and talking about what you're experiencing!

Guidelines for Digital Media:

  • Under 2 years: Minimize screen time; prioritize real interactions
  • Ages 2-5: Maximum 1 hour daily of high-quality content
  • Co-view and discuss: Watch together and talk about what you see
  • Choose quality content: Educational, age-appropriate, interactive
  • Balance with active play: Screen time should not replace physical activity and hands-on exploration
  • No screens during meals or before bed: Protect important family interaction times

Creating Rich Multimodal Experiences

Combining multiple modes for deeper learning

The most powerful learning experiences combine multiple modes! Here are examples of activities that naturally integrate different communication modes:

Story Acting

Modes used: Linguistic + Gestural + Visual + Audio + Spatial

How to do it:
  • Read or tell a story together
  • Assign roles to family members or toys
  • Act out the story with dialogue, movement, and props
  • Add sound effects and music
  • Take turns being different characters

Why it works: Engages the whole child and deepens story comprehension

Creating Story Books

Modes used: Linguistic + Visual + Gestural

How to do it:
  • Child draws pictures to tell a story
  • Child dictates words while adult writes them down
  • Read the book together with expression
  • Share with family members

Why it works: Child becomes the author, connecting drawing and writing

Musical Movement Stories

Modes used: Audio + Gestural + Linguistic + Spatial

How to do it:
  • Tell a story while adding movements and music
  • Example: "We're going on a bear hunt" with actions and sounds
  • Encourage child to suggest movements and sounds
  • Vary pace, volume, and movements to match story events

Why it works: Combines rhythm, movement, and language for memorable learning

Photo Story Projects

Modes used: Visual + Linguistic + Gestural

How to do it:
  • Take photos during an outing or activity
  • Look at photos together and talk about what happened
  • Put photos in order to create a sequence
  • Child tells the story using the photos
  • Create a simple photo book or poster

Why it works: Connects personal experiences with storytelling and sequencing

Tips for Supporting Multimodal Communication

DO:

  • Encourage children to express themselves in multiple ways
  • Provide diverse materials (art supplies, musical instruments, dress-up clothes, books)
  • Model using different modes yourself
  • Value all forms of communication, not just words
  • Create a language-rich environment with visuals, sounds, and textures
  • Follow children's lead and interests

DON'T:

  • Insist on "words only" communication
  • Discourage gestures, drawings, or other forms of expression
  • Rely solely on screens or digital content
  • Expect perfection in any mode
  • Limit creative expression to prescribed activities
  • Compare children's communication styles

The Power of Multimodal Communication

When we expand our view of communication beyond just spoken words, we open up a world of possibilities for children! Every child can find their voice—whether through words, gestures, art, music, movement, or a creative combination of all these modes.

By encouraging and celebrating multimodal communication, we help children become confident, creative, and effective communicators who can express themselves in rich and varied ways. That's the foundation for lifelong learning and success!
