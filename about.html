How Children Learn to Talk

Understanding the amazing journey of language development

Learning to talk is one of the most incredible achievements of early childhood! Children are born ready to communicate, and through loving interactions with family members, they gradually develop the skills to listen, understand, and express themselves through spoken language.

Listening Skills: The Foundation

Before children can speak, they need to listen! Listening skills begin in the womb and continue to develop rapidly during the first years of life. Babies are born ready to pay attention to human voices, especially the voices of their caregivers.

What Children Learn Through Listening:

  • Sound discrimination: Recognising different sounds, tones, and voices
  • Understanding words: Connecting sounds to meanings
  • Following instructions: Processing and responding to requests
  • Attention and focus: Concentrating on speakers and conversations
  • Turn-taking: Learning the rhythm of conversation
Did you know? Even newborns can recognise their mother's voice and prefer it over unfamiliar voices. This early listening lays the groundwork for all future language learning!

Speaking Skills: Finding Their Voice

Speaking involves much more than just making sounds! Children need to coordinate their breathing, vocal cords, tongue, lips, and jaw to produce clear speech. This complex process develops gradually through practice and interaction.

Key Speaking Milestones:

  • Cooing (2-3 months): Making vowel-like sounds "ooo," "aaa"
  • Babbling (6-10 months): Combining consonants and vowels "ba-ba," "da-da"
  • First words (12 months): Saying meaningful single words
  • Two-word phrases (18-24 months): "More juice," "Daddy go"
  • Sentences (2-3 years): Putting multiple words together
  • Clear speech (3-5 years): Speaking in full sentences that others understand
Family Tip: Every child develops at their own pace. Celebrate each new sound, word, and phrase as a wonderful achievement!

Vocabulary & Grammar Development

As children learn to speak, they're also building their vocabulary (the words they know) and grammar (the rules for putting words together). This happens naturally through everyday conversations and experiences.

How Vocabulary Grows:

Early Vocabulary (12-18 months)

  • Names of family members
  • Common objects (ball, cup, book)
  • Action words (go, eat, sleep)
  • Social words (bye-bye, hi)

Expanding Vocabulary (2-5 years)

  • Describing words (big, happy, cold)
  • Location words (in, on, under)
  • Time words (now, later, yesterday)
  • Feeling words (sad, excited, scared)

Grammar Development:

Children learn grammar rules by listening to how others speak. They start with simple patterns and gradually learn more complex sentence structures:

Stage 1: Single Words

"Milk!"

Meaning: "I want milk" or "Here is milk"

Stage 2: Two Words

"More milk"

Starting to combine words

Stage 3: Short Sentences

"I want more milk"

Using pronouns and connecting words

Stage 4: Complex Sentences

"Can I please have more milk?"

Using questions, polite words, and grammar rules

Conversational Turn-Taking

Conversations are like a dance—taking turns speaking and listening! This important skill helps children become effective communicators who know how to share ideas, listen to others, and engage in back-and-forth exchanges.

Why Turn-Taking Matters:

  • Builds social skills and friendships
  • Teaches children to listen actively
  • Helps children stay on topic
  • Develops patience and self-regulation
  • Prepares children for school discussions

Example of Turn-Taking in Action:

Adult: "What did you play with today?"
Child: "Blocks!"
Adult: "Wow! What did you build with the blocks?"
Child: "Big tower! It fall down!"
Adult: "Oh no! Did you build it back up again?"
Child: "Yes! Very tall!"

Notice: Each person takes turns, listens to the other, and builds on what was said. This is a natural, enjoyable conversation that helps the child practice important communication skills!

Phonological Awareness: Playing with Sounds

Phonological awareness is the ability to hear and play with the sounds in words. This skill is essential for learning to read and write! When children can identify rhymes, clap syllables, and hear individual sounds, they're building a strong foundation for literacy.

Key Phonological Skills:

Rhyming

Recognising and creating words that sound similar:

"Cat, hat, mat, sat"

Activity: Sing nursery rhymes and rhyming songs together!

Syllable Awareness

Breaking words into smaller chunks:

"But-ter-fly" (3 claps)

Activity: Clap out syllables in names and favourite words!

Beginning Sounds

Hearing the first sound in words:

"Dog, duck, dinosaur all start with 'd'"

Activity: Play "I Spy" with beginning sounds!

Sound Play

Making up silly words and sound combinations:

"Wibbly-wobbly, ziggy-zaggy"

Activity: Create funny nonsense words together!

Why This Matters: Children who develop strong phonological awareness in the preschool years find it easier to learn letter-sound relationships and decode words when they start reading. It's all about making language play fun!

Oral Language: Foundation for Reading & Thinking

Strong oral language skills are the secret to school success! Children who can listen well, speak clearly, use rich vocabulary, and engage in conversations are better prepared to learn to read, write, and think critically.

The Connection to Reading:

Listening

Understanding spoken language helps children understand written language

→

Vocabulary

Knowing more words makes it easier to recognise and understand them in books

→

Reading Success

Children with strong oral language become confident readers

The Connection to Thinking:

Language is also the tool we use for thinking! As children develop more sophisticated language skills, they can:

  • Solve problems by talking through options
  • Remember and retell experiences
  • Plan and predict what might happen
  • Express and understand emotions
  • Ask questions to learn new things
  • Make connections between ideas

The Bottom Line:

Every conversation you have with your child, every book you read together, every song you sing, and every question you answer is building their language skills AND preparing them for future learning. You are your child's first and most important teacher!
