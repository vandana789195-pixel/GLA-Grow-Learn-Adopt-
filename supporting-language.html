Supporting Language at Home

Practical strategies for nurturing communication skills every day

You don't need special equipment or expensive programs to support your child's language development! The most powerful tool you have is your everyday interactions. Through conversations, play, reading, and daily routines, you can create a rich language-learning environment right in your home.

Remember: YOU are your child's first and most important teacher. Every conversation, every song, every story makes a difference!

The Power of Adult–Child Interactions

Why quality conversations matter

Research consistently shows that children learn language best through warm, responsive interactions with caring adults. It's not just about HOW MUCH you talk, but HOW you talk with your child that makes the biggest difference.

What Makes Interactions Powerful?

Being Present

Get down to your child's level, make eye contact, and give them your full attention

Following Their Lead

Talk about what interests your child in the moment

Back-and-Forth

Taking turns—you talk, they respond, you respond back

Rich Language

Using varied vocabulary and complete sentences

Positive Tone

Warm, encouraging, and enthusiastic communication

Regular Practice

Consistent, daily opportunities to communicate

Key Strategies for Supporting Language

"Say It Better" educator-style examples

1

Ask Open-Ended Questions

Instead of questions that require only "yes" or "no" answers, ask questions that encourage your child to think and explain. This builds vocabulary and encourages longer responses.

Closed Questions

  • "Did you have fun at the park?"
  • "Is your teddy soft?"
  • "Do you want a snack?"

Open-Ended Questions

  • "What did you play at the park?"
  • "Tell me about your teddy. What does he feel like?"
  • "What kind of snack would you like?"
Try these question starters: "What...", "How...", "Why...", "Tell me about...", "What happened when..."
2

Repeat and Expand Children's Speech

When your child says something, repeat what they said and add a little more! This validates their communication, models correct grammar naturally, and introduces new vocabulary.

Example 1:

Child: "Dog!"

Adult: "Yes! That's a big brown dog. The dog is running!"

Why it works: Confirms the child's word and adds descriptive language

Example 2:

Child: "Car go fast"

Adult: "Yes, the red car is going very fast down the street!"

Why it works: Expands the sentence and adds details

Example 3:

Child: "I goed to park"

Adult: "That's right! You went to the park. What did you do at the park?"

Why it works: Models correct grammar without criticizing, then extends the conversation

Remember: Never correct by saying "No, say it this way." Instead, model the correct form naturally!
3

Shared Reading

Reading together is one of the BEST activities for language development! But it's not just about reading the words—it's about talking ABOUT the book together.

Interactive Reading Strategies:

Before Reading
  • Look at the cover together: "What do you think this story is about?"
  • Make predictions: "I wonder what will happen!"
  • Connect to experiences: "Have you ever seen a train like this?"
During Reading
  • Point to pictures and name things
  • Ask questions: "Where's the cat?" "What's the bear doing?"
  • Make sound effects and use different voices
  • Pause to let your child finish familiar phrases
  • Let your child turn the pages
After Reading
  • Talk about the story: "What was your favourite part?"
  • Retell the story together
  • Act out parts of the story
  • Connect to real life: "Just like when you..."
Book Selection Tips:
  • Choose books your child is interested in
  • Read the same favourites repeatedly—repetition helps learning!
  • Pick books with clear pictures and not too much text
  • Include books that represent your family's culture and language
4

Storytelling

You don't need a book to tell a story! Storytelling helps children learn narrative structure, sequencing, vocabulary, and creative expression.

Types of Storytelling:

Personal Stories

Tell stories about your day, childhood, or family history

"When I was little, I had a dog named Buddy. One day, Buddy..."

Made-Up Stories

Create imaginative stories together

"Once upon a time, there was a purple dinosaur who loved to dance..."

Photo Stories

Look at photos together and tell the story of what happened

"Look, here we are at the beach! Remember when the wave splashed us?"

Story Acting

Act out stories with toys, puppets, or dress-up

Use toy animals to act out "The Three Bears"

5

Play-Based Talk

Play is children's work! When you join in your child's play and talk about what's happening, you create wonderful language-learning opportunities.

How to Support Language During Play:

Narrate the Action

Describe what your child is doing

"You're stacking the blocks so carefully! That tower is getting tall!"

Pretend Play

Join in pretend scenarios and take on roles

"I'll be the customer. Can I please have some pizza? What toppings do you have?"

Give Voice to Toys

Make toys "talk" to encourage dialogue

"Teddy says, 'I'm hungry! Can you make me some breakfast?'"

Think Aloud

Share your thinking process

"Hmm, I wonder which puzzle piece goes here? Let me try this one..."

Language in Daily Routines

Every moment is a learning opportunity!

You don't need to set aside special "teaching time." Language learning happens naturally when you talk with your child during everyday activities!

Mealtime Talk

What to do:

  • Name foods and describe them: "These carrots are crunchy and orange"
  • Talk about tastes, textures, temperatures: "Is your soup hot or warm?"
  • Discuss preferences: "What's your favourite fruit?"
  • Practice manners: "Please pass the bread. Thank you!"
  • Share about your day: "Tell me about preschool today"

Example conversation:

"What would you like to eat?" → "I want apple" → "You'd like a red apple? Should I cut it into slices?"

Bath Time

What to do:

  • Name body parts: "Let's wash your arms, your legs, your toes!"
  • Describe actions: "Splash! The water is splashing everywhere!"
  • Use water play for concepts: "The cup is full. Now it's empty!"
  • Sing bath-time songs
  • Play with foam letters or bath toys
Vocabulary to introduce: wet, dry, clean, dirty, soap, bubbles, splash, pour, floating, sinking

Getting Dressed

What to do:

  • Name clothing items: "Let's put on your socks and shoes"
  • Describe colours and patterns: "Your shirt has red stripes"
  • Give simple instructions: "Arms up! Now put your head through"
  • Talk about weather: "It's cold today, so we need a jacket"
  • Offer choices: "Which shirt—the blue one or the green one?"
Vocabulary to introduce: on, off, over, under, zip, button, warm, cold, soft, scratchy

Bedtime Reading & Routines

What to do:

  • Read bedtime stories together
  • Sing lullabies and quiet songs
  • Talk about the day: "What was the best part of today?"
  • Discuss tomorrow: "Tomorrow we're going to..."
  • Use calming, gentle language

Bedtime routine:

Story → Song → Cuddles → "Goodnight" conversation → Sweet dreams!

Outdoor Exploration

What to do:

  • Name things you see: "Look at that bird! I see a tree, grass, flowers..."
  • Describe nature: "The leaves are rustling in the wind"
  • Compare sizes: "This rock is bigger than that one"
  • Talk about sounds: "Can you hear the birds singing?"
  • Encourage exploration: "What do you think is under this log?"
Vocabulary to introduce: grass, leaves, clouds, wind, insects, birds, flowers, trees, sky, ground

Shopping & Errands

What to do:

  • Name items: "We need milk, bread, and apples"
  • Describe what you're doing: "I'm putting the cereal in the cart"
  • Count together: "Let's get three bananas. One, two, three!"
  • Talk about colours and shapes: "Can you find something red?"
  • Give simple jobs: "Can you help me find the yogurt?"
Vocabulary to introduce: shopping, cart, shelf, checkout, basket, money, pay, heavy, light

Tips for Success

Make Time

Even 10-15 minutes of focused, screen-free interaction each day makes a huge difference

Minimize Screens

Face-to-face interaction is best for language development. Put away phones during conversations

Follow Their Interests

Children learn best when they're engaged! Talk about what excites them

Wait for Responses

Give your child time to think and respond. Don't rush to fill silences

Celebrate Attempts

Praise all efforts at communication, even if the words aren't perfect

Create a Language-Rich Home

Have books accessible, sing songs, play word games, and talk often!

The Most Important Thing

Language development doesn't require fancy toys or expensive programs. It thrives on loving, responsive interactions between children and their caregivers. Your attention, engagement, and enthusiasm are the most valuable gifts you can give your child!

Every conversation is a lesson. Every story is an adventure. Every question is an opportunity. Keep talking, keep reading, keep playing—you're doing an amazing job!
